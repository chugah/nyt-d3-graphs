    The New York Times Data Visualization Assignment

    By Sami Barghshoon
    September 26, 2017

    Objective

    The goal of this assignment is to visually display quantitative information through color, layering, and interaction in order to reach conclusions and make decisions. The information source stemmed from a NY Times JSON article feed — see protocol section for URL.

    Protocol

    The JSON feed was provided by the New York Times to track the life cycle for articles that appear in the section fronts. Data points were collected every hour for 24 hours. The first data point was collected Sunday Sept. 17th, 2017 at 6 PM and the last collected Monday Sept. 18th at 6 PM. The data was converted from JSON to csv via this online tool.Data visualization relied on the d3 JavaScript library.

    The fields used from the JSON feed include Id, Publication Date, Build Date, Title, Article Link, Author, and Kicker. The difference between the Publication and Build Dates determined the number of days an article appeared. A Category field was created and determined from the article link. For instance, the link for Id 100000005423118 contains 'science' after the publication date '2017/09/13' (see below). Therefore, this article fell into the science category.

    https://www.nytimes.com/2017/09/13/science/parasites-extinction-climate-change.html

    The analyses excluded the following Ids: 100000005148449, 100000005358680, 100000004718174, 100000005261282, and 100000005391163. Ids ending with 449 and 680 were removed because they were videos the analyses was restricted to articles only, However, the remaining ids were articles with publication dates as far back as October 2016. Since it was not possible to determine whether these articles appeared every day or how often since their publication date, they were omitted. Moreover, no statistical analyses were undertaken to the remaining data set due to the small sample size (less than 100).

    Limitations

    The following factors limited the scope of the analyses:

    • Data trend is directional at best
    • 24 hour cycle is not sufficient to identify larger trends
    • Article position on the web page was not considered
    • Click through rate influence could not be evaluated
    • Impact of image on the number of days article appeared
    • Difficult to implement interactive charts in real time

    While this list is not exhaustive, it warrants mentioning that further studies are needed to address article rotation and its impact on factors such as website traffic, ad placements, etc.

    Results

    The sample size obtained from this study was 98 articles. These articles were analyzed to determine the length of an article featured from its publication date to display an article's lifecycle during the 24 hour period. Each analysis utitlized a different visualization technique.

    Number of Days Article Featured

    The length of time an article featured was elucidated through animated bubble charts developed by Jim Vallandingham. The following pattern emerged from this analysis:

    • 10 articles were featured for more than 3 days
    • 20 articles were featured for 2 or more days
    • 44 articles were featured for more than 1 day
    • 56 articles were featured for less than 1 day

    60% of articles featured more than 2 days had a kicker associated with them compared to 25% of articles featured for less than 1 day. The earliest publication date observed was 9/13/2017 and the most recent publication date was 9/18/2017. Only 9 authors contributed to more than 1 article (data not shown).

    Bubble Charts in Real Time

    It is an arduous process to render bubble charts in real time. Significant algorithms are required to handle the force aspect of d3 to initiate and manage the animation. Writing these algorithms would take weeks.

    Efforts have been made to generate bubble charts through API use, but the motion lacked an organic quality. Tomomi Imura demonstrated d3 bubble animation using PubNub API and the resulting force of motion.

    24 Hour Cycle of Articles

    The 24 hour cycle of an article was examined through parallel coordinates technique developed by Mike Bostock, and bar graph with tooltip developed by Justin Palmer. The analysis revealed the following information:

    • 30 articles were removed over 24 hours
    • 36 articles were added over 24 hours
    • 7 articles appeared the entire 24 hours
    • 26 articles were added and removed over 24 hours
    • 2 articles were added, removed, and added again over 24 hours

    Overall, the removal and addition of articles followed a homeostatic process. Directionally, more articles appeared during the day than evening and the number of articles peaked on September 18th at 12 PM.

    Bar and Line Charts in Real Time

    Rendering a bar or line chart without animation is feasible. However, this feasibility is diminished greatly once animation is introduced.

    There are several examples online that use d3 to generate basic charts. Simen Brekken and Bo Ericsson demonstrated d3 animation using simple line chart and dot chart respectively.

    d3 Animation Data Tables

    Another challenge with d3 animation is the slightest error in data table format prevents the rendering of the visual. Therefore, a lot of backend scrubbing is required to ensure data visualization.

    Conclusions

    An analysis of the New York Times news feed revealed how long articles are featured and how frequently they change over 24 hours. It would be interesting to evaluate this pattern over a longer period and include factors such as an article's click through rate, placement on the web page, and use of image.

    Information gleamed from this exploration could be useful in its support of marketing activities, content management, and ultimately, revenue generation. It may also reveal the processes present or absent that govern article selection.
